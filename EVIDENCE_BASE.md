# Heat Acclimation via Passive Heat Exposure (Dry Sauna vs. Steam Room) for Half-Ironman (70.3) Preparation: An Evidence Base for a Garmin Connect IQ App

## TL;DR
- **Dry sauna and steam room are physiologically distinct stimuli — the steam room blocks evaporative cooling and so drives core temperature and heart rate up faster per degree of air temperature — but the adaptations they produce overlap heavily and transfer between dry and humid race conditions.** The single best controlled comparison (McDonald et al., *Journal of Physiology*, 2026, trained cyclists/triathletes) found dry and humid heat acclimation (HA) produced *similar* adaptations and strong cross-acclimation. The app should treat both modalities as drivers of one underlying adaptation, but weight them differently per minute, because the true driver is **core-temperature elevation and time held elevated**, not air temperature.
- **The app cannot measure the true dose (core-temperature time above ~38.5°C) directly, and the best wrist-available proxy is heart rate (absolute and as % of max) plus its within-session drift, supported by trended resting HR and HRV. Wrist skin temperature is the weakest proxy** because the skin-to-core gap is large and variable and skin temperature actually *leads* (does not lag) rectal temperature by ~12.5 minutes during heating, while commercial fusion algorithms still carry a 5–30 min lag.
- **A defensible protocol: ~30 minutes per session (or to thermal tolerance), 3–5 sessions/week for ~2–3 weeks to induce, then 2–3 sessions/week to maintain, finishing the most intense heat work ~7+ days before race day, because adaptations decay at roughly 2.3–2.6%/day.** Safety guardrails (hydration, session caps, contraindication screening) must *gate* the scoring, not merely inform it.

## Key Findings

1. **Core-temperature elevation is the adaptive driver; everything the wrist measures is a proxy for it.** The isothermic/controlled-hyperthermia literature targets a rectal temperature of ~38.5°C held for ~60 minutes. Between ~37.3°C and 38.5°C the magnitude of acclimation is proportional to the thermal "forcing function," but raising core temperature beyond 38.5°C does **not** confer additional benefit (Fox et al.; Gibson et al., 2015; reviewed in Akerman et al., *Frontiers in Physiology*). Passive sauna protocols in athletes raise core temperature by ~1.0–1.5°C (Stanley-type and elite cross-country skier work).

2. **Dry and humid heat acclimation produce similar adaptations and cross-acclimate.** McDonald et al. (*Journal of Physiology*, 2026; 12 endurance-trained male cyclists/triathletes, 10 completing both arms) directly compared hot-dry vs. warm-humid HA and concluded environment-specific differences were "overshadowed by the overall adaptive stimulus" (controlled-HR HA). Blood volume, plasma volume, red-cell volume, and haemoglobin mass changed similarly between conditions. The authors' practical recommendation: use dry heat to prepare for *both* dry and humid heat, optionally "habituating to humid heat in the final few acclimation sessions if such conditions are anticipated." Drier conditions also better preserved training quality (greater work rate at the same HR/core temperature/RPE). **Evidence flag: trained athletes.**

3. **The two literatures can largely be POOLED for the underlying adaptation but must be WEIGHTED separately for thermal strain per minute.** In a steam room (~40–49°C / 110–120°F, ~95–100% relative humidity) sweat cannot evaporate, so evaporative cooling is near-zero and core temperature and heart rate rise faster and higher *per degree of air temperature* than in a dry sauna (~70–100°C, 5–30% RH). Pilch et al. (*Biology of Sport*, 2014; ten healthy men aged 25–28, three 15-minute rounds) found that despite the wet steam bath being *cooler*, rectal temperature, heart rate, the Physiological Strain Index, and the Cumulative Heat Strain Index all rose significantly more than in the hotter dry sauna, while body-mass (sweat) loss was about twice as large in the dry sauna (−0.72 kg dry vs −0.36 kg wet). The authors concluded the wet sauna "is more stressful for the organism than the dry sauna, where the temperature is higher with low humidity."

4. **There is essentially NO direct research on literal steam rooms as a heat-acclimation *training* stimulus.** A targeted search found no peer-reviewed study using a steam room / Turkish bath / hammam in a repeated-exposure protocol to induce and then *test* thermoregulatory adaptations (plasma volume, lowered sweat threshold, reduced exercising HR/core temperature) in athletes. The steam-room literature is almost entirely acute single-session physiology (e.g., Pilch 2014; Shoenfeld et al., *Arch Phys Med Rehabil*, 1976) or wellness/clinical studies. The validated passive HA modalities are dry sauna, post-exercise hot-water immersion (HWI), and humid *climate chambers* — steam rooms do not appear as a studied passive HA modality in the Périard/Sawka-type passive-heating review (*Frontiers in Physiology*, 2018, 9:1851). Commercial "steam therapy for athletes" claims frequently misattribute dry-sauna studies (e.g., Scoon 2007, Stanley 2015 — both dry sauna) to steam rooms. **The app must flag all steam-room guidance as extrapolated / lower-confidence.**

5. **Dose-response: both the number of exposures and per-session duration matter; minimum effective dose is roughly 5+ sessions.** Daanen, Racinais & Périard (*Sports Medicine*, 2018) report that ≥5 HA days induce stable end-exercise HR and core-temperature adaptations. Tyler et al. (meta-analysis 2016; updated *J Science in Sport & Exercise*, 2024) classify short-term (≤7 days), medium (8–14), and long-term (>15) HA, with long-term protocols producing the most robust sweat-rate adaptations (overall pooled effect on performance/capacity g ≈ 0.7; resting core temperature g ≈ −0.6; HR g ≈ −0.5). McDonald et al. (*Comprehensive Physiology*, 2025; Bayesian meta-regression of 211 papers, mean protocol 8±4 exposures, 90±36 min/exposure, 39.1°C) quantified incremental dose effects: HA lowered end-exercise HR by −17 bpm [−19, −14], end-exercise core temperature by −0.43°C [−0.48, −0.36], and expanded plasma volume by 5.6% [3.8, 7.0]; each additional 15 min/exposure further lowered end-exercise core temperature by −0.04°C and expanded plasma volume by 0.4%, and each additional exposure raised whole-body sweat rate and haemoglobin mass. **Evidence flag: predominantly exercise-HA, mixed trained/recreational.**

6. **Adaptation markers an app can trend: plasma volume expansion (~5–7%), reduced HR at a given heat load, lower resting HR, earlier sweat onset, and rising HRV.** Scoon, Hopkins, Mayhew & Cotter (*J Sci Med Sport*, 2007; six male distance runners, post-exercise humid sauna 89.9±2.0°C for 31±5 min, 12.7±2.1 sessions over 3 weeks, max HR 140±11 bpm) reported run time to exhaustion increased 32% (90% CL 21–43%) — equivalent to ~1.9% in a time trial — with plasma volume up 7.1% (5.6–8.7%) and red-cell volume up 3.5%, and performance change strongly correlated with plasma-volume change (r = 0.96). HRV falls acutely with heat exposure then rises chronically with adaptation, and resting HR falls (Flouris/Buchheit-type field data and *European Journal of Applied Physiology* HRV studies showing significant bradycardia and marked HRV changes across induction/decay). **Evidence flag: Scoon = small, all-male trained runners.**

7. **Decay is rapid: ~2.3–2.6%/day; finish hard heat work ~7 days out and maintain with 2–3 sessions/week.** Daanen et al. (*Sports Medicine*, 2018): end-exercise HR adaptation decays 2.3%/day (P<0.001) and end-exercise core temperature 2.6%/day (P<0.01) for every day without heat; "1 day of HA is lost following 2 days" of decay. Plasma-volume-linked cardiovascular adaptations decay fastest; sweat-rate adaptations from longer programs are more durable. This is the single most decision-relevant number for race-week tapering logic.

8. **Timing: post-workout heat sessions are efficient and lower-risk than standalone hard heat exercise.** Passive heat (sauna/HWI) applied after a normal session adds thermal dose without much additional mechanical or cardiovascular load. Medium-term post-exercise HWI conferred *more complete* acclimation than exercise-HA "without increasing overreaching risk" (PMC9602919), whereas intensified exercise-HA can trigger functional overreaching and disrupt sleep (Osborne et al., 2023; *IJSPP* 2017 high- vs low-intensity HA). Recommendation across practitioner sources: avoid stacking heat on hard-training days, prefer non-consecutive or easy-day-adjacent scheduling, and cap to ~2 heat sessions in the final pre-race week.

9. **Sweat-rate estimation via the sauna is valid and directly useful for race hydration planning.** Weigh nude before and immediately after (before drinking): every 1 kg of body-mass loss ≈ 1 L of fluid lost. Endurance sweat rates commonly range ~0.5–2.0 L/h (occasionally to ~3 L/h); sweat sodium concentration is roughly 800–1200 mg/L (~1 g salt per litre). Use these to set individual race fluid and sodium targets — but never aim for 100% fluid replacement, which risks hyponatremia.

## Details

### Central question (a): Do adaptations transfer between dry sauna and steam room?
Yes. The strongest evidence (McDonald et al., *J Physiol*, 2026) shows dry and humid HA induce similar adaptations with robust cross-acclimation in trained cyclists/triathletes. Older crossover work (Nielsen et al., 1993/1997, reviewed in Périard et al., *Autonomic Neuroscience*, 2016) found comparable plasma-volume expansion (~9–13%) under both dry (40°C/20% RH) and humid (35°C/85% RH) HA, although dry HA additionally produced greater stroke-volume (~21 mL/beat) and cardiac-output (~1.8 L/min) gains that humid HA did not. Senay et al. (*J Appl Physiol*, 1976) documented that in humid HA, plasma volume rose ~9% by day 2 and called PV expansion "the most critical event in heat acclimatization." Net: the *core* adaptations transfer; some central hemodynamic gains may modestly favour dry heat. **Evidence flag: McDonald = trained; Nielsen/Senay = mixed/healthy adults.**

### Central question (b): Is one modality better for a humid vs. dry race?
Specificity exists but is small and partially contested. A heat-index- and time-matched short-term crossover (dry 43°C/20% RH vs humid 32°C/80% RH; *IJSPP*, 2020) found differing minor adaptations — plasma volume was retained 3–4 days longer after humid HA, while dry HA slightly reduced gross mechanical efficiency. The peripheral sweat-gland literature suggests sweat glands "become resistant to fatigue so that higher sweat rates can be sustained, particularly in humid climates" (Gonzalez/Nadel 1974, in Périard et al., 2016), i.e., humid exposure may preferentially train sudomotor endurance. The pragmatic consensus (GSSI/Sawka-Périard SSE #153; McDonald 2026): dry heat is a robust general preparation for both environments; add humid/steam exposure in the final sessions to polish for a humid race. **For a 70.3 in humid conditions, the steam room's blocked evaporative cooling more closely mimics race-day thermal load** — a specificity argument, not a magnitude-of-adaptation argument.

### Central question (c): Pool or weight separately in the app?
**Pool** for the underlying adaptation (plasma volume, exercising HR, core temperature, sweat onset) — the literatures converge. **Weight separately** for *thermal strain per minute*: because evaporative cooling is near-zero in ~100% humidity, a minute in a steam room generally drives core temperature up faster than a minute in a dry sauna at typical settings (Pilch 2014). The scoring model should therefore **not** treat "minutes in heat" as equivalent across modalities; apply a modality multiplier (steam-room minutes worth more thermal strain at equivalent thermal comfort), while acknowledging this multiplier is derived from acute physiology, not from acclimation-outcome data.

### Measurable wrist proxies and the skin-to-core problem
- **Heart rate is the best single wrist proxy for thermal/cardiovascular strain.** During passive heat, HR climbs to roughly 100–150 bpm (Scoon dry sauna: max ~140±11 bpm); HR rises with core temperature and is the primary input (with skin temperature) to commercial core-temperature estimation algorithms.
- **Skin-to-core gap and timing:** wrist/skin temperature runs ~1–2°C below core at rest, and the relationship is highly variable with peripheral vascular tone, ambient temperature, and anatomy — a 0.5°C wrist change does **not** map to a fixed core value (consumer-wearable validation literature). During heating, skin temperature and HR *lead* rectal temperature: Eggenberger et al. (2018, *Frontiers in Physiology*, 9:1780) found via cross-correlation that "12.5 min [is] the average delay of peak rectal temperature compared to the peak skin temperatures and heart rate." Commercial fused core-temperature devices (e.g., CORE/greenteg) still describe a 5–30 min lag and degraded accuracy below 34°C skin temperature; an independent validation (*Journal of Thermal Biology/Applied Ergonomics*, 2025) found the CORE sensor overestimated core temperature by ~0.3°C in late-stage exercise and overestimated skin temperature by ~1.3°C, with bias related to subcutaneous fat.
- **Cardiovascular/HR drift:** a progressive HR rise during steady heat exposure indexes rising thermal strain; well-trained and acclimated individuals show smaller drift (typically <5% in the fit). Drift onset can precede core-temperature inflection (PSU HEAT project, *J Appl Physiol*, 2023), making it a usable early strain signal.
- **HRV:** falls acutely with heat then rises chronically with adaptation; best used as a *trended* readiness/adaptation marker rather than a single-session dose measure, given its day-to-day noise and parasympathetic-only sensitivity (RMSSD).

### Target endpoint and its wrist translation
True laboratory target: core ~38.5°C held ~45–60 minutes. Because the wrist cannot measure this, the best obtainable surrogate is **sustained heart rate in a moderate-to-vigorous band (roughly the 100–140+ bpm / ~65–80% HRmax range observed in sauna studies) maintained for the session, combined with a meaningful within-session HR drift** indicating progressive heat storage. The app should explicitly present this as a proxy, not a core-temperature measurement, and should not write to Garmin's proprietary Heat Acclimation metric.

### Duration, frequency, program length, decay
- **Session:** ~30 minutes (Scoon: 31±5 min at 89.9±2.0°C) or to thermal tolerance.
- **Frequency:** 3–5×/week to induce. Consecutive days accelerate adaptation but accumulate fatigue.
- **Program:** cardiovascular adaptations largely within the first week; sweat adaptations require ~10–14+ days; "complete" adaptation often takes >2 weeks and is individually variable (the "myths and methodologies" critique notes the classic "1 week" claim is partly an artefact of study designs).
- **Decay:** ~2.3–2.6%/day (Daanen 2018). Maintain with 2–3 sessions/week; finish intense heat work ~7 days before the race and carry light maintenance into race week — balancing the conflict between HA's "complete within 7 days of relocation" guidance and the taper principle of minimizing fatigue ~14 days out.

### Markers to trend ("is it working?")
Plasma-volume expansion (proxied by falling HR at a fixed heat load and falling resting HR), earlier sweat onset, rising resting HRV, and improved thermal comfort. A simple in-app check: compare HR at a fixed easy pace (or fixed sauna minute) on day 1 vs day ~12 in similar conditions — a clear drop signals plasma-volume adaptation. Flag a plateau or reversal.

### Timing relative to training
Post-workout sauna or HWI is the efficient, lower-risk integration that adds thermal dose without large extra mechanical load and tends not to provoke overreaching. Avoid stacking heat onto hard-training or sleep-deprived days; ensure HR and (estimated) thermal load return toward resting baseline before the next session; cap to ~2 heat sessions in the final pre-race week.

### Sweat rate and sodium
Weigh nude before/after (1 kg ≈ 1 L), ideally across several sessions and conditions to build an individual profile. Combine with a known/assumed sweat sodium (~800–1200 mg/L) to estimate hourly sodium loss and set race targets. Sauna sweat-rate testing is a legitimate, controlled way to generate this data when outdoor heat is unavailable.

### Safety and contraindications (must gate scoring)
- **Hydration:** pre-load ~500 mL 1–2 h before; ~150–250 mL between rounds; rehydrate to ~150% of measured losses afterward (≈60% within the first 30 min). Acclimation *raises* sweat/fluid and sodium needs.
- **Session caps:** novices start at 5–10 min and build gradually; ~30 min is a reasonable upper bound for trained users; immune/illness/fever days should be 5–10 min max or skipped.
- **Heat-illness red flags (exit immediately):** dizziness, nausea, headache, confusion, disorientation, or cessation of sweating.
- **Contraindications (avoid or require medical clearance):** recent myocardial infarction (wait ~3–6 months), unstable angina, decompensated heart failure, severe aortic stenosis, acute infection/fever; pregnancy — especially the first trimester (neural-tube-defect risk when core temperature exceeds ~39°C/102°F in weeks 4–10); alcohol before/during; medications impairing thermoregulation (beta-blockers, diuretics, some psychiatric drugs).
- **Never** combine aggressive heat with pre-existing dehydration or hard-training fatigue. (Finnish mortality data identify alcohol + sauna as a leading precipitant of sauna-related cardiac events.)

### Race-climate matching
The evidence for modality-specific matching is real but modest. Dry heat is a robust general preparation for both dry and humid races; humid/steam exposure adds race-specific polish for a humid 70.3 and better replicates the blocked-evaporation thermal load of race day. This should be a *recommendation nudge*, not a strong scoring penalty for the "wrong" modality.

## Recommendations
1. **Model dose as core-temperature-time, proxied by HR-time-in-zone (and within-session HR drift), not raw minutes.** Apply a modality multiplier so steam-room minutes accrue more thermal-strain credit than dry-sauna minutes at equivalent thermal comfort — but cap the steam multiplier conservatively and label it extrapolated, since it rests on acute physiology (Pilch 2014), not acclimation outcomes.
2. **Stage the program:** an induction block (~2–3 weeks, 3–5 sessions/week, ~30 min each) scored against accumulating thermal-strain dose, then a maintenance score (2–3×/week) once trended markers plateau. **Threshold to switch to maintenance:** HR-at-heat-load and resting HR have fallen and stabilized over ~10–14 days.
3. **Trend resting HR, HR-at-heat-load, resting HRV, and estimated sweat rate as adaptation signals.** **Benchmark that confirms adaptation:** a clear drop in HR at a fixed sauna minute/easy pace between day 1 and ~day 12; rising resting HRV. **Benchmark that should trigger a "not working / increase dose or check recovery" prompt:** no HR drop after two weeks of consistent exposure (commonly because sessions were too short/cool to sustain a sweat response) — or, conversely, rising resting HR + falling HRV + poor sleep, which should trigger a *reduce-load* prompt (overreaching risk).
4. **Hard-code the decay model (~2.5%/day) and prompt the athlete to finish intense heat work ~7 days pre-race, with light maintenance (1–2 short sessions) into race week.** If the athlete stops entirely >2 weeks out, warn that most adaptation will be lost by race day.
5. **Gate all scoring behind safety logic:** mandatory hydration prompts, escalating session caps, a one-time contraindication screen, automatic load reduction (or session block) on flagged hard-training/poor-recovery/illness days, and a hard ceiling on session duration and weekly heat frequency.
6. **Label steam-room scoring as lower-confidence/extrapolated** in the UI and in any coaching copy, and present HR-based dose as a proxy — never as a core-temperature measurement.

## Caveats
- **No direct studies use literal steam rooms for HA training.** Steam-room guidance is extrapolated from acute steam physiology (Pilch 2014; Shoenfeld 1976) plus dry-sauna, HWI, and humid-chamber acclimation data. Humid-chamber exercise-HA (80–85% RH) is the closest validated analogue but is not the same as passively sitting in ~100% RH steam.
- **Wrist skin temperature is a weak core-temperature proxy** (large, variable skin-to-core gap; leads core during heating yet device fusion adds 5–30 min lag; fat- and ambient-dependent bias). HR is better but still indirect; the app should avoid implying it measures core temperature.
- **Most robust dose-response data come from exercise-HA**, not passive heat; passive protocols are studied in smaller samples (e.g., Scoon n=6, male; elite-skier sauna studies small) and the strongest dry-vs-humid comparison (McDonald 2026) used male cyclists/triathletes. Female-specific and 70.3-specific dose-response data are limited; note that whole-body sweat-rate increases with humid HA are larger in men than women (Avellini et al.).
- **The "humid > dry for plasma volume" idea is hedged in its sources and contradicted by recent matched comparisons** (McDonald 2026; Nielsen) showing similar plasma-volume expansion (~9–13%) in both — so the app should not over-favour steam on a plasma-volume rationale.
- Decay and induction kinetics are population averages with large individual variation; the app should personalize off each user's own trended markers rather than applying fixed numbers rigidly.
